<p class="phylo-lede"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phylogeny">Phylogeny</a> is the conceptual and practical framework for studying biodiversity from an evolutionary perspective. My research program unifies hypothesis testing with data-driven approaches in phylogenetics to uncover the factors that generate evolutionary patterns, a concept described as “Phylogenetic Natural History” (<a href="https://academic.oup.com/sysbio/article/67/6/1091/4985805">Uyeda <em>et al.</em> 2018</a>).</p>

<section id="phylo-inference" class="phylo-module">
  <h2>Phylogeny Inference</h2>

  <p>Resolving the evolutionary history of groups that rapidly diversified remains a fundamental challenge in systematic biology. My interests in this area are motivated by the extinction of non-avian dinosaurs and the subsequent diversification of vertebrate clades around the K-Pg boundary (~66 Ma).</p>

  <figure class="phylo-figure phylo-figure--wide">
    <a href="https://doi.org/10.1038/nature15697">
      <img src="/images/research/Prum-Bird-Evolution.jpeg" alt="Bird phylogeny visualization from Prum et al. 2015" />
    </a>
  </figure>

  <p>In one flagship example, we investigated relationships among modern bird families and estimated a new timescale of avian diversification (<a href="https://jakeberv.com/publication/2015-10-07-Prum_Berv_et_al_2015">Prum, Berv, et al. 2015, <em>Nature</em></a>). We leveraged target-capture DNA sequencing to infer nine super-ordinal groups of modern birds and advanced approaches for <a href="https://academic.oup.com/sysbio/article/68/1/145/5043533">phylogenetic experimental design</a>, including phylogenetic informativeness metrics for improving study power.</p>

<section id="phylo-divergence" class="phylo-module">
  <h2>Divergence Time Estimation</h2>

  <p>A longstanding issue in systematic biology is conflict between inferred ages from fossils versus molecular sequences. For birds, fossils often imply key divergences close to the K-Pg boundary (~66 Ma), while molecular clocks can suggest substantially older ages.</p>

  <figure class="phylo-figure phylo-figure--wide">
    <a href="https://doi.org/10.1093/sysbio/syx064">
      <img src="/images/research/Field_et_al_2020-scaling.png" alt="Model diagram related to divergence-time estimation bias" />
    </a>
  </figure>

  <p>In <a href="https://jakeberv.com/publication/2018-01-01-Berv_Field_2018">Berv and Field 2018, <em>Systematic Biology</em></a>, we tested whether a K-Pg-associated acceleration of avian genomic evolution contributes to this discrepancy. The hypothesis builds on the “Lilliput effect”: if mass extinctions favor smaller-bodied taxa and smaller birds evolve faster genomes, molecular clocks may systematically overestimate ages for major avian divergences.</p>

<section id="phylo-tempo-mode" class="phylo-module">
  <h2>Tempo and Mode of Molecular Evolution</h2>

  <p>As a Life Sciences Fellow at the University of Michigan (2019-2023), I explored how variation in both evolutionary tempo (rate) and mode (process) can confound phylogenetic inference. In work available <a href="https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2022.10.21.513146v1">as a bioRxiv preprint</a>, I examined lineage-specific molecular model heterogeneity across crown birds and found multiple model shifts concentrated near lineages originating after the end-Cretaceous extinction.</p>

<section id="phylo-deepsea" class="phylo-module">
  <h2>Habitat-Associated Molecular Evolution in the Deep Sea</h2>

  <p>In <em>Evolution</em> (2025), Amelia Weiss and I tested whether habitat longevity predicts rates of molecular evolution in deep-sea specialists. Using mitochondrial CO1 data from bathymodioline mussels and siboglinid tubeworms, we compared lineages occupying organic falls, hydrothermal vents, and cold seeps.</p>

  <p>Organic falls, hydrothermal vents, and cold seeps persist over dramatically different timescales (from decades to millennia), imposing hard limits on ecological persistence and selecting for distinct life-history strategies.</p>

  <p><strong>Key findings:</strong></p>
  <ul>
    <li>Substitution rates show an inverse relationship with habitat longevity.</li>
    <li>Organic-fall specialists evolve fastest; vent and seep lineages are slower.</li>
    <li>After phylogenetic correction, body size does not explain rate differences.</li>
  </ul>

  <figure class="phylo-figure phylo-figure--wide">

    </a>

  </figure>

  <p>Because the same pattern appears in two deeply diverged phyla, the results support a broader resource-longevity framework: ecological persistence can shape life-history strategy and the tempo of molecular evolution.</p>
